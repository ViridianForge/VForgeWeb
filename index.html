The analysis of electromyographic (EMG) data was a major component of the research done by Drs. Rachwani and Santamaria in their study of reaching behavior in typically developing infants and children severely affected by cerebral palsy.

To facilitate their research, I developed and implemented two lines of software in the MATLAB environment. The first was an analytic suite developed to the researchers specifications that extracted a series of statistical measures from the EMG. The second, pictured to the right, was a graphical aid to enable the researchers to easily identify muscle activations in a type of EMG where identifying muscle activity is typically quite difficult.

When analysing electromyographic (EMG) data, electrocardiographic (EKG) artifact present in the data collection can make it difficult to accurately assess muscle activity. When Dr. Sandy Saavedra and I first approached this problem in 2008, the solutions we were aware of either allowed too much EKG to remain in the data, or removed more EMG data than we were comfortable with. To this end, we created a software suite based on work in <>'s paper <> in the MATLAB environment. The software isolated the time locations of heart beats based on the EMG channel with the strongest EKG artifact and then used this information to create an EKG wave for each EMG channel that was then removed.

When Drs. Rachwani and Santamaria began replicating the methodology in Dr. Saavedra's study, I revisited this work. Working very closely with the researchers, we overhauled both the functionality and design of the system, yielding a system that was more robust and usable.

In her thesis work, Dr. Sandy Saavedra engaged in the analysis of the kinematics of reaching in both typically developing infants and children with varying severities of Cerebral Palsy. Using MATLAB, we worked together to produce a suite of tools to expedite the researcher's abilities to easily visually inspect this data, and generate statistical analyses of the data in multiple dimensions.

Numerous researchers continued pursuing this line of research, and I worked with them to incrementally improve upon both the design and functionality of the software, extending to incorporate research notes from coded videos of associated reaches to isolate the exact begin and end of reaching events.
